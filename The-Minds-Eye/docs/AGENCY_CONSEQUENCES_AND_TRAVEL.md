# Student Agency, Consequences, and Travel

## Core Principle

Students should be able to create new quest branches through their choices.

The simulation should not be a fixed railroad. Students can investigate, travel, question people, make mistakes, cause problems, solve problems, and trigger consequences.

The teacher remains in control, but student agency should feel real.

## Student-Generated Quest Branches

A student or team may trigger new branches by:

- asking an unexpected question
- visiting an unplanned location
- helping a historical person
- insulting or bothering a historical person
- damaging property
- trying to sneak into a restricted location
- refusing a civic responsibility
- finding a clue early
- making a strong inference
- making a false accusation
- choosing to travel to another town or nearby settlement

## Consequence Engine

The system should model consequences that are historically plausible, instructionally useful, and age-appropriate.

Consequences should teach civic systems, ethics, cause and effect, geography, historical context, and social responsibility.

### Example Consequence

If a student harasses, threatens, or seriously disrupts a townsperson inside the simulation:

1. the local sheriff may be notified
2. the student/team may be detained inside the story world
3. the class may need to speak with a judge
4. students may need to present evidence, apologize, pay a fine, repair harm, or explain their intentions
5. the teacher can use this as a civic/legal learning moment

This should not shame real students. It is a story-world consequence with teacher control.

## Consequence Categories

- social reputation
- law enforcement involvement
- court/judge hearing
- loss of time
- access denied
- NPC distrust
- fine or restitution inside the simulation
- repair task
- public apology
- additional evidence requirement
- redirected mission objective
- community service style task

## Teacher Controls

Teachers must be able to:

- approve or deny consequence escalation
- soften consequence language
- pause the simulation
- redirect students
- convert misconduct into a teachable moment
- prevent one student from ruining the class session
- reset a branch if needed

## Travel Mechanics

Travel should be a major system, but not branded after any copyrighted tabletop product.

Students should be able to travel:

- within a Sanborn map sheet
- between stitched Sanborn sheets
- across town
- to nearby creeks, bridges, depots, farms, schools, churches, and industrial sites
- to nearby towns when the historical geography supports it

## Travel Variables

Travel can consider:

- distance
- time of day
- weather
- road/rail access
- walking, horse, wagon, rail, ferry, or boat
- student role abilities
- supplies
- safety risks
- map certainty
- source confidence

## Travel Outcomes

Travel checks can create:

- arrival on time
- delay
- lost route
- unexpected witness
- weather problem
- animal/wagon issue
- railroad schedule conflict
- discovery of bonus location
- civic encounter
- missed deadline

## Dice-Based Resolution

Dice rolls should create uncertainty, but they should not replace mastery.

Use dice for:

- travel complications
- witness cooperation
- finding a clue quickly
- persuading a public official
- avoiding a delay
- discovering a bonus source
- managing limited resources

Do not use dice to decide whether a student understands the TEKS.

## Teacher-Facing Consequence Summary

For every branch, the system should show:

- what student action triggered it
- why the consequence is historically plausible
- what academic opportunity it creates
- what standard can be assessed
- whether teacher approval is required

## Student-Facing Tone

Consequences should feel immersive, not punitive.

Bad:

> You are punished for being rude.

Better:

> Word travels quickly in town. The livery owner refuses to answer more questions until your team repairs the harm and explains your purpose.

## Non-Negotiable

Student agency is not chaos. The system must combine freedom, historical plausibility, teacher control, and standards-based evidence.
