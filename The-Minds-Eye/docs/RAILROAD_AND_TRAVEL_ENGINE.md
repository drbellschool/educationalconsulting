# Railroad and Travel Engine

## Purpose

Railroads are a major part of The Mind's Eye.

In many late 19th-century Texas towns, trains shaped commerce, migration, communication, conflict, opportunity, and daily life. The railroad should not be scenery. It should be a world system.

## Core Functions

The railroad engine should model:

- train arrivals and departures
- passenger movement
- freight movement
- mail and newspapers
- livestock transport
- special events
- delays and accidents
- commerce effects
- new quest hooks
- travel to nearby towns

## Train Types

The system should support historically appropriate train types, including:

- passenger train
- freight train
- mixed train
- mail/express car
- baggage car
- livestock car
- boxcar
- flatcar
- coal/wood/fuel car
- refrigerated car where historically appropriate
- circus train
- excursion train
- maintenance/work train

Each type can generate different classroom opportunities.

## Train Arrival Effects

A train arrival can bring:

- new passengers
- newspaper issues
- telegrams
- mail
- freight goods
- livestock
- circus performers and animals
- political speakers
- traveling salesmen
- illness concerns
- witnesses
- suspects
- missing persons
- business inventory
- rumors
- weather/news from elsewhere

## Quest Hooks from Trains

Examples:

- a mailbag is missing
- a shipment is mislabeled
- a passenger needs help finding family
- livestock escaped from a car
- a circus train arrives and overwhelms town services
- a shipment is delayed and a business runs short
- a newspaper from another city changes local understanding
- a witness arrives for court
- a rail worker reports damage to a bridge

## Travel Mechanics

Students may travel:

- on foot
- by horse
- by wagon
- by rail
- by boat/ferry where appropriate

Travel variables:

- distance
- cost
- time
- weather
- road condition
- rail schedule
- availability of tickets
- inventory carried
- reputation with depot staff
- historical safety conditions

## Travel Outcomes

Travel can result in:

- arrival as planned
- delay
- missed train
- route complication
- unexpected witness
- lost item
- weather problem
- new clue
- need for lodging
- additional cost

## Source Requirements

Whenever possible, railroad data should come from:

- Sanborn maps showing depots, sidings, and rail yards
- newspapers
- timetables
- railroad maps
- city directories
- historical photographs
- local histories

If exact train data is unavailable, generated train activity must be labeled as simulation_element or creative_license.

## TEKS Connections

Railroad events can support:

- geography
- economics
- industrialization
- civic infrastructure
- cause and effect
- migration
- supply chains
- reading informational texts
- argument and evidence
- math through time, distance, cost, and schedules

## Teacher Controls

Teachers should be able to:

- enable/disable travel
- restrict travel to town limits
- approve out-of-town travel
- control travel risk level
- select whether train arrivals can trigger new quests
- approve special events like circus trains

## Non-Negotiable

Railroad events must remain historically plausible and clearly labeled by source status.
