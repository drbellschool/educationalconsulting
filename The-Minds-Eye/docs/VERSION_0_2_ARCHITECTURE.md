# Version 0.2 Architecture

## Product Identity

The Mind's Eye is a historical world engine for classrooms.

It should not be treated as a simple quest generator. Quests emerge from a data-rich historical simulation made from maps, people, institutions, transportation, commerce, law, community life, and primary sources.

## Core Pillars

1. Historical Source Engine
2. Sanborn Map Builder
3. World Engine
4. Railroad and Travel Engine
5. Commerce Engine
6. Community and Institution Engine
7. Justice and Civic Consequence Engine
8. Inventory and Evidence Engine
9. TEKS and Mastery Engine
10. Teacher, Student, and Community Dashboards
11. Citation, Provenance, and Review System

## Updated Product Rule

The archive is the ground.

The map is the world.

The railroad brings change.

Commerce gives the world motion.

Institutions generate needs.

Students create branches through choices.

Teachers approve instruction.

Community reviewers verify local accuracy.

AI proposes, but does not become truth until reviewed.

## Every Object Needs Provenance

Almost everything created or used in the system should include provenance metadata.

This includes:

- people
- places
- buildings
- events
- quests
- rumors
- train arrivals
- inventory items
- prices
- court actions
- church concerns
- commerce tables
- student-generated branches
- images
- NPC statements

## Provenance Classifications

- verified_source: directly supported by a cited source
- source_based_inference: reasonable inference from sources
- simulation_element: historically plausible instructional addition
- creative_license: invented for gameplay and clearly labeled
- student_generated: created by student action during play
- teacher_approved: reviewed or edited by teacher
- community_approved: reviewed by local/community reviewer

## Student Agency

Students can create new situations by their actions.

Examples:

- they help someone and gain trust
- they insult someone and lose access
- they interfere with a person and draw attention from the sheriff
- they travel and trigger route complications
- they discover a contradiction in sources
- they request a train ticket or freight manifest
- they visit a church, depot, court, newspaper, livery, school, or business
- they create a new civic problem that must be solved

## Consequences

Consequences should be immersive, age-appropriate, civic-minded, and teacher-controlled.

A poor decision inside the simulation may lead to:

- sheriff involvement
- court hearing
- witness testimony
- restitution
- loss of time
- reputation damage
- denied access
- repair mission
- apology or mediation
- extra evidence requirement

The goal is not punishment. The goal is historical cause and effect.

## The Town Remembers

The simulation should maintain a world ledger.

If students help a merchant, insult a judge, discover a bridge problem, damage property, solve a church concern, or recover a lost item, that action can be recorded.

Teachers may choose:

- single-session reset
- class campaign memory
- persistent town memory for a unit
- archived historical simulation runs

## Non-Negotiables

1. No unreviewed AI extraction becomes final map content.
2. No generated fiction is presented as verified history.
3. Teacher approval is required before student-facing launch.
4. Community review adds trust but cannot silently rewrite primary sources.
5. Dice and chance mechanics create engagement, not academic mastery.
6. Mastery is based on evidence tied to TEKS.
